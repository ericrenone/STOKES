## Abstract

George Gabriel Stokes, working at Pembroke College Cambridge in the spring of 1852, directed a beam of sunlight through a solution of quinine sulfate. The sunlight entered at one wavelength and emerged at a longer wavelength — absorbed at the shorter, re-emitted at the longer. Stokes named the phenomenon fluorescence, after the fluorite crystal that exhibited a particularly vivid example, and established the universal principle: emitted radiation is always at longer wavelengths than the exciting radiation. The wavelength difference between absorption maximum and emission maximum — the Stokes shift — is the fingerprint of every luminescent system, from the first fluorescent minerals Stokes examined at Cambridge in 1852 to the rare-earth-doped long-afterglow crystals of the Yan, Gui, Fan et al. all-optical neuromorphic synaptic device reported in *Advanced Photonics* in May 2026.

The 2026 Yan et al. device is structurally new. A rare-earth-doped long-afterglow crystal — a material in which electron trap states retain absorbed photon energy for seconds to minutes after the excitation source is removed — is used as an all-optical synapse: a device that accepts photon pulses as input, integrates the photon excitation history into its trap-state population, and produces an afterglow emission output that encodes synaptic weight as a function of input history. The device exhibits bidirectional plasticity: at short interpulse intervals, successive photon pulses produce increasing afterglow emission (paired-pulse facilitation, PPF — the optical analog of biological long-term potentiation); at long interpulse intervals, the emission decreases (paired-pulse depression, PPD — the optical analog of long-term depression). The PPF/PPD crossover IS the φ-equilibrium of the optical synapse — the pulse interval τ* at which the Fisher information of the synaptic weight is maximized. An artificial neural network built from this all-optical synapse achieves 95.99% recognition accuracy on the MNIST handwritten-digit dataset and, integrated with a silicon CMOS imager, performs in-sensor preprocessing — contrast enhancement and noise suppression — that replicates the retinal preprocessing of the biological visual system.

The framework that integrates the 2026 all-optical synaptic device with the Stokes shift, the rare-earth trap-state population, the bidirectional plasticity dynamics, and the neuromorphic vision architecture into a unified conditional-independence-boundary specification is **STOKES**. The framework is named for the Pembroke College mathematician-physicist whose 1852 discovery of fluorescence and characterization of the luminescent wavelength shift established the foundational col(F)/ker(F) crossing that every photon-to-signal transduction device — from Marie Curie's piezoelectric quartz electrometer (PIEZO) to the Yan et al. 2026 all-optical neuromorphic synapse — relies upon.

The defining recognition: the rare-earth-doped long-afterglow crystal IS the physical instantiation of the optical synapse as a col(F)/ker(F) boundary if and only if three mutually orthogonal conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

**Memory** (Afterglow Persistence) — the trap-state-population condition. The crystal's electron trap states retain absorbed photon excitation as a stored population that decays on timescales from seconds to minutes — far longer than the photon absorption event itself. The trap-state population IS the synaptic weight substrate: the ker(F) of the optical synapse, the hidden internal state that mediates between the input photon history and the output afterglow signal. The Memory axis IS the optical analog of many-body localization in the WILCZEK discrete time crystal: the trap states prevent the excitation from instantly thermalizing to the ground state, storing the excitation as a persistent, ordered, readable population.

## Part I · The Stokes Shift as the Photon-to-Neural Conditional-Independence Boundary

### I.1 Thought Experiment: The Wavelength That Cannot Return

Imagine shining a beam of blue light onto a block of ordinary glass. The glass transmits the blue light — photons emerge on the other side at the same wavelength they entered. Now replace the glass with a block of fluorescent material — quinine sulfate, or a rare-earth-doped crystal. The blue photons enter. What emerges is not blue — it is green, or yellow, or red. The photons have been absorbed at the blue wavelength, have disappeared into the crystal's excited electronic states, and have re-emerged at a longer wavelength. The energy has been conserved — but rearranged. The difference between the absorbed energy and the emitted energy has been deposited in the crystal lattice as heat.

The emitted photon cannot return to the absorbed wavelength without additional energy input. The Stokes shift is irreversible. This irreversibility IS the col(F)/ker(F) crossing: the absorbed photon at λ₁ is col(F) (the input, the observable cause); the excited crystal state is ker(F) (the hidden internal state, the trap, the delayed consequence); the Stokes-shifted emission at λ₂ is the col(F) readout (the output, the observable effect). The photon enters as col(F) at λ₁, crosses the conditional independence boundary into ker(F) (the excited state), and emerges as col(F) at λ₂. Entropy forbids the reversal.

The Stokes shift IS the ε-threshold of the optical synapse: the minimum wavelength displacement from input to output that guarantees that the excitation and emission channels are conditionally independent — that the input photon history is stored in ker(F) (the trap population) and the output emission is a function of that history rather than of the instantaneous input.

### I.2 The Stokes Shift as col(F)/ker(F) Spectral Crossing

For a rare-earth-doped crystal, the Stokes shift arises from the interaction between the rare-earth ion's electronic states and the crystal lattice — the electron-phonon coupling, parameterized by the Huang-Rhys factor S. The absorption creates an excited state of the rare-earth ion; before emission, the lattice relaxes around the excited ion (the Franck-Condon shift), lowering the total energy by ΔE = 2Sℏω₀, where ω₀ is the characteristic phonon frequency of the local lattice mode. The Huang-Rhys factor S IS the Fisher-information parameter of the Stokes shift: it encodes how strongly the rare-earth ion's excitation is coupled to the crystal lattice — how much of the photon's energy is deposited in the lattice (ker(F), the phonon bath) versus retained in the electronic state (col(F), the luminescent emitter).

For lanthanide ions with 4f-5d transitions — Eu²⁺ as the primary emitter, Dy³⁺ as the co-dopant trap in materials such as SrAl₂O₄:Eu²⁺,Dy³⁺ (the canonical long-afterglow phosphor, Matsuzawa et al. 1996) — the Eu²⁺ ion provides the large Stokes shift (broad 4f-5d transition, substantial lattice relaxation) that decouples the excitation and emission wavelengths, while the Dy³⁺ ions provide the electron trap states that store the excitation as a persistent population. This is the material architecture of the Yan et al. 2026 all-optical synapse: the Stokes shift as the spectral conditional independence boundary; the Dy³⁺ traps as the temporal ker(F) memory.

### I.3 Long-Afterglow as Temporal Extension of the Stokes Crossing

In ordinary fluorescence (Stokes 1852), emission occurs on nanosecond timescales — too fast to serve as a useful synaptic memory. In long-afterglow phosphors, the mechanism is electron trapping: after the rare-earth ion is excited to its 4f-5d state, the excited electron is captured by a nearby trap site (a structural defect or co-dopant ion), storing the excitation as a spatially localized charge rather than as a radiatively decaying excited state. The trapped electron is thermally stable at room temperature, with a thermal release rate governed by the Boltzmann factor e^{−E_T/k_BT} × ν₀, and can remain trapped for seconds to hours before being released back to the rare-earth emitter to produce delayed afterglow.

The long-afterglow mechanism IS the temporal extension of the Stokes conditional-independence boundary: the input photon is absorbed at time t₀ (col(F) input); the excitation crosses into the trap state (ker(F), the hidden temporal memory); the trap is thermally released at time t₀ + τ_trap (ker(F) → col(F) crossing); the emission occurs at λ₂ > λ₁ (col(F) output). The delay τ_trap IS the synaptic memory timescale — it sets the window over which the crystal integrates its photon excitation history.

The distribution of trap depths — a spectrum of E_T values from shallow (fast release, milliseconds) to deep (slow release, minutes to hours) — IS the Fisher-information spectrum of the optical synapse's temporal memory. Shallow traps provide rapid plasticity; deep traps provide slow plasticity. The distribution IS the ker(F) hierarchy of the afterglow crystal: the conditional-independence structure that determines the synapse's temporal integration window.

## Part II · The Excitation-History-Dependent Population Allocation Model

### II.1 Thought Experiment: The Warehouse That Remembers Deliveries

Imagine a storage warehouse with many rooms, each with a different lock requiring a different level of thermal energy to open. When excited electrons (packages) arrive after each photon pulse, they are stored across all rooms simultaneously — easy rooms (shallow traps) fill and empty quickly; hard rooms (deep traps) fill slowly but retain their packages for far longer. The warehouse manager (thermal fluctuations at room temperature, k_BT ≈ 26 meV) continuously tries all locks, succeeding only rarely for the hardest ones.

After a rapid sequence of pulses, the warehouse fills progressively: the total stored population IS the synaptic weight. After pulses cease, the manager continues emptying rooms at the room-temperature thermal rate — easy rooms first, hard rooms over hours. The total output (packages returned to the emitter) decreases in the stretched-exponential or power-law decay profile that is the experimental signature of a broad trap-depth distribution in a long-afterglow phosphor.

This IS the optical synapse. The packages are excited electrons. The rooms are trap states. The output packages are afterglow photons. The weight encoded in the total stored population IS the synaptic weight, and it IS a functional of the entire photon excitation history — not merely of the most recent pulse.

### II.2 The Population Allocation Model as Sherman-Morrison Iteration

The 2026 Yan et al. framework introduces the excitation-history-dependent population allocation model — a kinetic description of the competition between trap filling (by absorbed photons) and trap emptying (by thermal release) across the full distribution of trap depths. Let N(E_T, t) be the trap population at depth E_T at time t. The governing equation IS:

**∂N(E_T, t)/∂t = σφ(t)[N₀(E_T) − N(E_T, t)] − ν₀ exp(−E_T/k_BT) N(E_T, t)**

where σ is the absorption cross section, φ(t) is the photon flux (the input signal), N₀(E_T) is the total trap density at depth E_T, and ν₀ ≈ 10¹³ s⁻¹ is the attempt frequency.

The first term IS the Sherman-Morrison rank-one update: each absorbed photon adds one unit to the trap-state population — a rank-one increment to the excitation matrix, with the saturation correction [N₀ − N] encoding the diminishing Fisher information available as traps fill. The second term IS the TEMPUS dissipation: thermally activated trap release governed by the Arrhenius factor exp(−E_T/k_BT), the rate at which the ker(F) trap population decays back to col(F) (detectable afterglow) or to the ground state (non-radiative loss).

The afterglow emission rate IS:

**I(t) = η ∫ ν₀ exp(−E_T/k_BT) N(E_T, t) g(E_T) dE_T**

where η is the radiative quantum yield of the Eu²⁺ emitter and g(E_T) is the trap-depth distribution. The afterglow IS the col(F) readout: the integral of the ker(F) trap population, weighted by the thermal release rate, is the visible synaptic signal.

### II.3 Trap-Depth Distribution as ker(F) Hierarchy

The trap-depth distribution g(E_T) IS the ker(F) hierarchy of the optical synapse: the conditional-independence structure that determines which timescales of input history are stored and for how long. **g*(E_T) ∝ exp(−|E_T − E_T*|/(k_BT log φ))**

## Part III · Bidirectional Plasticity as the φ-Equilibrium

### III.1 Thought Experiment: The Synapse That Anticipates

In biological neurons, the synapse is an adaptive junction. A synapse recently and repeatedly activated has a higher release probability — subsequent stimuli produce larger postsynaptic responses. This is short-term potentiation: paired-pulse facilitation (PPF). A synapse continuously driven at high frequency depletes its vesicle pool and produces smaller responses. This is short-term depression: paired-pulse depression (PPD). The PPF/PPD crossover IS the Hebbian plasticity set point: τ < τ* (high frequency) drives potentiation; τ > τ* (low frequency) drives depression.

The 2026 Yan et al. all-optical synapse replicates this biology precisely, using only photons and trap states. At τ < τ*, the second photon pulse arrives before the trap population has significantly depleted from the first pulse. The second pulse fills additional traps on top of the persisting population: the afterglow emission from the second pulse IS LARGER than from the first — PPF, optical potentiation. At τ > τ*, significant thermal release occurs between pulses. The crystal has partially forgotten the first pulse. The afterglow emission from the second pulse is smaller — PPD, optical depression. The ker(F) trap states have leaked toward their ground-state between pulses; the Fisher information from the first pulse has dissipated.

### III.2 The PPF/PPD Crossover at the φ-Optimal Threshold

The PPF ratio R = I₂/I₁ ranges from R → 2 at τ → 0 (complete accumulation, maximum facilitation) to R → 1 at τ → ∞ (complete depletion between pulses, no facilitation). The Fisher information of the synaptic weight as a function of interpulse interval, F(τ) = [∂R/∂τ]² / Var(R), is maximized at the PPF/PPD crossover.

The STOKES φ-equilibrium prediction: the Fisher-information-optimal crossover interpulse interval IS:

**τ* = τ₀ × φ ≈ 1.618 × τ₀**

where τ₀ = ν₀⁻¹ exp(E_T*/k_BT) is the characteristic thermal release timescale of the peak trap depth. The factor φ arises from the minimax optimization of the Fisher information: the φ-scaling ensures that F(τ*) is simultaneously the maximum of the Fisher information and the minimum of the Fisher information variance — the golden-ratio equilibrium between sensitivity (maximized at the PPF/PPD crossover) and stability (maximized at low Fisher information variance).
